Does the wrapper around an LLM matter more than the model itself?

Kirill Korikov · May 2026

Most LLM benchmarks hold the agent architecture constant and vary the model. We did the opposite: same models, four different agent wrappers, 15 text quests. The results were messier than the neat story I wanted.

The setup

Give an LLM a text adventure: read the situation, pick from a list of actions, repeat for 10-50 turns until you win or lose. Now do the same thing four different ways:

  • Baseline (A) - bare prompt, just pick a number
  • Reasoning (B) - structured chain-of-thought before choosing
  • Knowledge (C) - reasoning plus domain hints about game mechanics
  • Planner (D) - multi-turn planning with strategy updates

The quests come from Space Rangers, a corpus of community-authored text adventures with branching narratives and non-obvious optimal paths. They were not designed for LLM evaluation, which is part of why they work: the difficulty is organic, not synthetic.

We tested six production models across 15 quests and four wrappers. Each cell has one run in the current public leaderboard, so treat this as a v1.0 slice, not a final statistical claim.

What we found

Knowledge hints beat raw reasoning, but barely

Lightweight knowledge hints (C) reached 21.1% success, ahead of prompted reasoning (17.8%) and the bare baseline (16.7%). The effect is real enough to care about, but not big enough to declare victory.

Agents get stuck in loops

The traces are full of agents revisiting the same states and re-picking familiar actions. Loop detection is still the most obvious cheap early stopping signal, but v1.0 reports it as a diagnostic, not a proven predictor.

Different architectures help different problem types

No wrapper dominates. Knowledge helps a little on average, planning is not automatically better, and some quests remain mostly unsolved. The useful signal is per-quest, not one magic architecture.

Cost-performance tradeoffs are real

DeepSeek, GPT-5.4, and GPT-5.4 Mini tie at 20% success in this slice. That is the annoying but useful lesson: wrapper changes and quest variance can swamp small model differences.

The practical takeaway: run the ablation before buying a bigger model. In this benchmark, the wrapper matters, but the effect is jagged. Architecture helps, then the quest punches it in the mouth.

The failure taxonomy

Reading the traces showed four recurring failure shapes. These are not solved yet; they are the next places to attack:
